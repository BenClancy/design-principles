Government Digital Service Content principles

  1. How we write

    We speak to the user in their language. We give them the information they want to know and we make sure they find it quickly.

    The voice of GOV.UK

    Our writing is:

  2. serious but not pompous
  3. in the vernacular ;-)
  4. active and engaging
  5. trustworthy and official
  6. definitive and authoratitive
  7. incisive (but still human)
  8. brief, succinct and to the point

    9. No beaten bushes here!

    Writing concisely We don't confound them with extra information, however useful we might think that is. , free of jargon, plain english, no caveats, no unwieldy grammar, taking risks? "You can" not "you may be able to"

  10. Content design

    We analysed our content needs and found we needed a suite of content formats. These different formats allow us to effectively answer user needs by designing content that's clear, rummageable and gives people confidence that they've got the right answer.

    Guides

  11. Don't force a linear reading pattern
  12. Don't duplicate information but also don't assume people have read all parts of the guide

    13. Answers

    Answers assume prior knowledge and answer a popular - and specific - need (so you do not need to explain terms or provide context).

    Stick exclusively to the answer - don't be tempted to include extra information, however useful this might appear to be.

  13. Answer summary - one sentence summing up the answer for common case - get the basic information in this summary - not an introduction - use summary tags
  14. Body - expand on summary with any necessary detail - be strict with information you include - don't stray off topic - stick to task - light touch on words even if this means compromising caveats - no need to repeat info in subhead
  15. Subheads - as close to search terms as possible - No need for the 'if' (eg 'You're working abroad') - H2 are primary subs
  16. Formatting - don't break up text with too many subheads - use formatting options (eg callouts) but not gratuitously - use accessibility formatting

    17. When does an answer become a guide?

    • When the answer can't be simplified - eg too many variables - such as 'What hours your child can work' - for this, the answer depends on too many things, such as the age of your child and the type of job
    • When it can't be summed up
    • When it's low popularity search - it can form part of a longer guide so it doesn't clog up the results page

    Benefits and schemes

    • Overview - give people the main things they want to know without going into detail - eg rate and how long it's paid for - separate out key points (not one long paragraph)
    • Main sections - stick to the title / need - resist temptation to provide information around the subject (especially superfluous advice) - use examples for complex calculations (use example markdown) - stick to common case - any edge cases get put in 'Further information'
    • Further information - this isn't a dumping ground - content still needs to be useful - if you can't find a place for it, suggest not using it - key information for this section: effect on other benefits, appeals and edge cases - also more information on how things are worked out
