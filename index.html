  1. Government Digital Service Design Principles

  2. Build for inclusion

    Our primary concern - everyone who needs and wants to use our services finds it as easy as possible to do so. We should not be afraid to make things obvious. We should not redesign common web practises that already work.

    Tips and techniques

    • Make it readable - never under 16pt.
    • Remember people scan, not read.
    • Set expectations clearly (what happens if I click this?).

  3. Iterate wildly

    The best way to build effective services is to start small and iterate wildly. Release products early, test them with real users, move from Alpha to Beta to Launch adding features and refinements based on feedback from real users.

    Tips and techniques

    • Design in browser.
    • Encourage feedback - build easy ways for people to talk to you.
    • Build with the future in mind - don’t trap yourself in a dead end.

  4. Get to the
    quick do

    Don’t waste people’s time. These are often services people have to use so get them to the thing they have to do as quickly as possible. Optimise for the common case but don’t forget the exceptions.

    Tips and techniques

    • Start with the most important thing on the page.
    • Optimise for the common case - but don’t forget the exceptions.
    • Remove any design feature that distracts from the core purpose.

  5. Consistent not uniform

    We need consistency so that people don’t have to relearn how to use different sites and to encourage trust in our services. However, this consistency shouldn’t be at the expense of simplicity, clarity etc. Build consistent experiences not identical ones.

  6. Do the work to make it simple

    We should make things simple for the user - this will often make things difficult for us.We have to do the hard work involved in hiding complexity, in redesigning processes to make them simple and comprehensible.

    Tips and techniques

    • Always maintain state.
    • Always make help contextual.
    • Always prioritise simplicity over beauty.
    • Focus the design on one thing at a time.

  7. Start with needs

    The design process must start with identifying and thinking about real user needs. Design around those - not around the way the ‘official process’ is done. Make sure you understand those needs thoroughly and remember that what users ask for is not always what they need.

    Tips and techniques

    • Know your users - they won’t be like you.

  8. Design with data

    The core advantage of designing digital services is that we can watch them being used in great detail and constantly improve them.This means measuring everything useful and acting on it - to test shape our content, to test our assumptions and to iterate.

    Tips and techniques

    • Test with real users.

  9. Do less

    We’ll make better services by concentrating on the irreducible core. Government should only do what only government can do.

    Tips and techniques

    • Do as little design as possible.
    • Link to things rather than build them.
    • Let others build what you can’t.

  10. Context

    We must remember that we’re designing for people in the real world, not screens.We must consider the contexts of use - devices, capabilities, circumstances - and remember that people use the web as a whole, not just our sites.

    Tips and techniques

    • Google is the homepage, Facebook is where the people are - today.
    • Context changes - keep an eye on it.
